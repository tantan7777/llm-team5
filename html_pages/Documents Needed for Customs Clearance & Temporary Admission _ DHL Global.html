The documents you need for clearing customs

Shipping internationally? To ensure a smooth customs clearance process, it's essential to understand at least the basics of the paperwork involved. In this article, we’ll walk you through the key documents needed to clear customs, helping you avoid delays and get your goods to their destination on time.

What is customs clearance and why is it important?

The customs clearance process is the official procedure that allows goods to legally enter or exit a country. At the heart of this process is accurate information and documentation. 

When you import or export goods, it’s important that you provide a complete and accurate Commercial Invoice, along with the additional documentation required by the export/import country authorities. 

Based on the Commercial Invoice and additional documentation provided, the customs broker will then submit to customs authorities a customs declaration – which tells authorities exactly what is being exported/imported.

Therefore, it’s essential to provide a complete and accurate Commercial Invoice and other documentation, to avoid your shipment being held up, flagged for inspection, or even seized – causing costly disruptions to your business and delays for your customers.

The documents needed to clear customs

When shipping internationally, proper documentation (as per the export and import regulatory requirements) is key to ensuring your goods clear customs without delays. Each document plays a specific role in verifying what’s being shipped, where it’s coming from, and how it’s being transported. Check our full guide to the documents needed for international shipping to learn more.

7 common customs clearance delays – and how to avoid them

For a smooth customs clearance process, beware of these pitfalls:

1. Incomplete or inaccurate documentation

Missing, inaccurate or unclear information on documents like the Commercial Invoice or Air Waybill can cause delays. Always provide complete and accurate information, compliant with regulatory requirements of the export and import countries.

2. Incorrect Tariff Classification Code(s)

Wrong Tariff Classification Codes slow down clearance and affect duty calculations. Use official tools from customs authorities to determine your Tariff Classification Code. You can also leverage DHL’s MyGTS for guidance regarding potential tariff classification code for your goods.

3. Undervaluing goods

Declaring a lower value to reduce duties is illegal by customs authorities, and can result in delays, significant fines, penalties, or additional consequences. Always state the true value of the goods and provide proof or purchase/payment if needed.

4. Non-compliance with regulations

Failing to follow a country’s import/export rules can lead to holds, seizures, fines and penalties. Research requirements and secure any needed permits, licenses or certificates in advance.

5. Unpaid duties or taxes

Customs won’t release your goods until the applicable regulatory charges are paid. Decide upfront who pays –shipper or receiver – and use fast payment options.

6. Improper labeling or packaging

Poor or missing labels, especially for hazardous goods, cause delays. Follow all packaging rules and ensure labels are clear and correct.

7. No response to customs queries

If customs authorities or DHL request additional information or documentation and don’t receive a timely reply, your shipment can be delayed, so always respond quickly to any queries.

Temporary admissions

Understanding how temporary shipments are handled is just as important as knowing the standard customs clearance process, especially if your business regularly moves equipment or samples across borders.

Temporary admissions typically allow goods to enter a country for a limited time, generally without paying import duties or taxes, as long as the items are not sold and are re-exported within a set period, in accordance with the local regulatory requirements. 

Please note that there are strict conditions and documentation requirements that must be adhered to when it comes to temporary admissions, so please ensure to research the export/import regulations.

Temporary imports

These are goods brought into a country for short-term use, such as tools for a specific project, commercial samples, or equipment for testing. These items must leave the country within a defined timeframe and cannot be sold or altered significantly. Proper documentation and approvals are essential to qualify for duty exemptions under temporary import rules. 

Please note that there are strict conditions and documentation requirements that must be adhered to when it comes to temporary imports, so please ensure to research the export/import regulations.

Temporary exports

Temporary exports involve sending goods out of a country with the intention of bringing them back later. This applies to items like equipment sent abroad for repair or use at an event. To avoid additional duties upon return, exporters must clearly document the temporary nature of the export and follow all customs requirements to ensure smooth re-entry, in accordance with the local regulatory requirements.

Please note that there are strict conditions and documentation requirements that must be adhered to when it comes to temporary imports, so please ensure to research the export/import regulations.

The importance of ATA Carnets

ATA Carnets allow companies to move eligible items – such as trade show displays or professional equipment – across multiple countries typically without paying duties or taxes, as long as the goods return within the carnet’s validity period, as per the country’s regulatory requirements. Widely accepted in over 80 countries, ATA Carnets save time and money by speeding up the customs clearance process. Please note that not all goods are eligible for the ATA Carnet process, and specific types of goods may require additional documentation or compliance checks.
 

Get expert support for your import and export process

  • Pre-shipment planner: a dedicated tool to help your business understand some of the applicable import/export regulations.

  • Landed Cost calculator to enhance your pricing strategy and give your customers transparency over the estimated shipping costs – which builds trust with your business. 
  • Guidance on import and export requirements: understand some of the country's import and export requirements before you ship – to avoid goods being held up at borders!

It’s essential that you keep up to date with the import and export regulatory requirements of the countries where you ship from/to, and consult with customs experts or legal advisors when necessary to ensure compliance with the regulations.
